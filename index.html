All possible pain and salvation

Chapter 15 constraints on Perfection
I have been trying to show the advantages and disadvantages of adaptation theory, but the main purpose of this chapter is to make a list and classify various constraints on perfection, and to list the main reasons why students should move forward cautiously in the process of learning adaptability. Before discussing my six constraints on perfection, let’s talk about the other three constraints that others have put forward, but I find they are not very convincing. First, the debate on “neutral mutation” among contemporary biochemical geneticists has been cited in the criticism of adaptation theory, but in fact, the two are irrelevant. If there are neutral mutations called by biochemists, it means that any changes in protein structure caused by these mutations have no effect on protein enzyme activity. This means that these neutral mutations will not change the process of embryonic development, nor will they have any phenotypic effects at all - the phenotypic effects understood by biologists as a whole. The biochemical debate on neutrality is mainly concerned with an interesting and important question: whether all gene substitutions have phenotypic effects. The debate on adaptation theory is quite different. Its main concern is that since the phenotypic effect we study is large enough to be observed and raises questions about it, we should assume that it is the product of natural selection. Biochemists’ neutral mutation is not just neutral. As long as we biologists who study rudimentary morphology, physiology and behavior consider this problem, they are no longer mutations in our eyes. It is with this idea that Maynard Smith wrote: “I interpret the ‘rate of evolution’ as the rate of adaptive change. In this sense, the substitution of a neutral allele does not constitute evolution. “ If a biologist who studies biological individuals sees a phenotypic difference determined by genes, he already knows that he cannot be studying the “neutrality” debated by a contemporary biochemical geneticist.
The second one, which is proposed for the perfection restriction, considers the problem of allometric growth: “for deer of the subfamily Cervus, the growth rate of antler size is much faster than that of body size… So that larger deer have disproportionately large antlers. So there is no need to find a clear reason for the huge antlers on the big deer’s head. “ Of course, Levantin’s remark illustrates some problems, but I prefer to restate it. According to his words, the allometric growth rate should be a constant, as if it was an unalterable thing designated by God. However, constants on one time scale may also change on another time scale. Allometric growth constant is a parameter in embryonic development. Like any other such parameter, it may be dominated by genetic changes and may therefore change during evolution. In this way, levintin’s statement is similar to the following statement: all primates have teeth, which is just a fact about primates, so there is no need to give a clear adaptive reason for primates having teeth. However, what he really wants to express may be something similar to the following.
Leventin’s view on gene pleiotropy is related to another problem, which I should discuss here first. The problem is the definition of what he calls natural “suture” - the “phenotypic unit” of evolution. Sometimes the double effects of a gene are inseparable in theory. They are different sides of the same thing. Just as Mount Everest once had two names, it depends on which side you look at it. The oxygen carrying molecules in the eyes of a biochemist may be red in the eyes of behaviorists. But there is also a more interesting gene pleiotropy, in which the two phenotypic effects can be separated. The phenotypic effect of any gene (relative to its allele) is not the private property of the gene itself, but can be revealed with the help of the embryonic environment in which it plays a role. This provides a full opportunity for the phenotypic effect of one mutation to be changed by other effects, and also provides a basis for some excellent ideas, such as Fisher’s theory of dominant evolution, Medawar and Williams’ theory of aging, and Hamilton’s theory of Y chromosome embedding. In this connection, if a mutation has a favorable effect and a harmful effect, there is no reason why selection does not favor a modifier gene, which can separate the two phenotypic effects, or weaken the harmful effect and enhance the beneficial effect. As in the case of allometric growth, leventin’s view of gene behavior is too static, treating pleiotropy as the private property of the gene itself, rather than as the result of the interaction between the gene and its modifiable embryonic environment.
Time lag
The animals we see today are likely to be “outdated”, and the genes that affect their establishment process were selected at an earlier time to cope with different conditions from today.
Historical constraints
My own experience is that there may be a solution to the real paradox of historical constraints. The analogy of jet engine implies that animals should be funny giants made up at random under the spur, with strange relics left by patchy antiques in their unstable bodies.
Available genetic changes
No matter how strong a potential selection pressure is, it will not necessarily lead to evolution unless there is a genetic change that makes the selection pressure work.
Nature does not have the foresight to combine a series of mutations to put a strain on the path to the ultimate global optimum - even if they may lead to temporary shortcomings. Nature cannot restrain itself from favoring the available mutations that can bring small advantages at present, even if they can get greater benefits from the optimal mutations that will appear later. Like a river, natural selection blindly improves its downward path along an immediately selectable continuous route with minimal obstacles. It can be imagined that the animals obtained from this are not the most perfect design, or even just barely survive. It is the product of a series of historical changes, and each change at most represents the option that happened to be better at that time.
Cost and material constraints
I think the above hypothesis is more constructive and interesting than the hypothesis that “the necessary mutation never appeared”. Of course, we have to admit that the former hypothesis may also be true, but I prefer to just take it as the last straw.
Imperfection caused by the selective action of another level at one level
One of the main problems to be solved in this book is the level at which natural selection plays a role. The adaptability we should see when selection acts on the level of biological groups will be quite different from the adaptability we expect to see when selection acts on the level of biological individuals. It can be concluded that what one individual selectionist regards as adaptability may not be perfect in the eyes of another group selectionist.
Errors caused by unpredictability or “malice” of the environment
No matter how well an animal adapts to the environment, these environmental conditions must also be regarded as a statistical average. It is usually impossible for us to deal with every conceivable accident in detail, so any given animal will often be observed to make a “mistake” - easily fatal. This is not the same as the time lag problem already mentioned. The time lag problem arises because of the uncertainty of environmental statistics: the current average conditions are different from those experienced by the ancestors of animals. The problem here is even more inevitable. Modern animals may live under the average conditions consistent with their ancestors, but the possible events in the environment they face are also different every day. These situations are too complex to make accurate prediction.
Such cases always make us wonder why these organisms still obey these manipulations against their best interests on the time scale of evolution? Why doesn’t selection simply erase the Robin’s vulnerability to cuckoo deception? I believe that such problems and many other problems will one day become the basis for a new branch of Biology - the science of manipulation, arms race, and phenotype.

Text

Chapter 13 gene extension
There is a disturbing contradiction at the core of selfish gene theory, which exists between genes and the carrier of life - life. On the one hand, we have got a beautiful story: independent DNA replication factors are as flexible as antelopes. They are freely handed down from generation to generation, temporarily combined in disposable biological containers, and the immortal double helix is constantly reorganized and inherited, honed when forming a decaying body, and finally move towards their own eternity. On the other hand, if we only observe the individual life itself, every life is a self-contained instrument, which is perfect, complex and precise, but unified and closely organized. Life is not just a product of a loose and temporary combination of genes. When sperm and eggs are about to start a new gene mixing process, these “Warring” gene vectors do not just know each other. With a dedicated brain, life coordinates the cooperation between limbs and sensory organs to complete various biological purposes. As a carrier, its work has reached the acme.
I think the most important question for any parasite is whether it transmits its genes to its offspring in the same way as the host’s genes. If not, I think it damages the host in various ways. But if they are the same, what a parasite can do is help its host survive and reproduce. With the progress of evolution, it will no longer be a parasite, but will cooperate with the host, and even eventually integrate into the host tissue. It is completely unable to identify it as a parasite. As I mentioned in Chapter 10, our cells have gone through this evolutionary process. We are actually the relics of all ancient parasitic biosynthesis.
Again, the main point here is: the genes of parasitic organisms need to pursue a common destiny and enjoy common interests with the host genes, so that parasitic organisms will eventually stop parasitic behavior.
One way to remind us is that even today, the phenotypic effects of not all genes are limited to the individual organisms in which they are located. In theory and in practice, genes cross the boundaries of individual organisms and manipulate objects in the external world, including inanimate things, living organisms and distant things. With a little imagination, we can see that genes sit in the center of the extended phenotypic radiation network. Any object in the world is at the node of this influence network, which comes from many genes in many organisms. There is no obvious boundary between the coverage of genes. The whole world is a cross, a causal arrow pointing to phenotypic effects by aggregated genes, either far or near.
There is another phenomenon: these crosses are gradually gathering. In fact, this phenomenon is very important and difficult to be ignored as an incidental phenomenon, but it is not enough to have a thorough foothold in theory. Replication factors no longer roam freely in the ocean, but gather into a huge group - individual organisms. Phenotypic changes are no longer evenly distributed in practice, and in many cases aggregate in the same body. We are familiar with the individual creatures on earth, but none of them ever existed on earth. No matter where in the universe, the only thing life needs is immortal replication factors.
Chapter 14 gene determinism and gene selection theory
What did genes do to have such an evil and irresistible reputation? Why don’t we demonize nursery education or confirmation courses into similar monsters? Why is it that only genes are considered more certain and irresistible than television, nuns or books? Ladies, don’t blame your husband for going out and fooling around. It’s not their fault to be stimulated by pornographic culture! The so-called Jesuits often boast, “give me your child’s first seven years and I’ll give you a man back.” That may make sense. Under certain conditions, the influence of education or other cultures may be as unalterable and irreversible as the influence of genes, and more people believe that “stars” have such influence.
I suspect that the reason why genes have become the monster of determinism is partly due to the confusion caused by a well-known fact, that is, the non heritability of learned characteristics. Before this century, it was widely believed that a person’s life experience and other knowledge gains could be imprinted on genetic material in some way and passed on to children. Later, people abandoned this cognition and replaced it with Weisman’s theory of germplasm continuity and its corresponding theory “central law” at the molecular level, which is one of the great achievements of modern biology. If we are in the inference of the orthodox theory of Weismann genetic school, genes do seem to be immutable and irresistible. They have been handed down from generation to generation, affecting the body that can’t escape death from generation to generation in form and behavior. However, apart from those rare nonspecific mutation effects, genes are never affected by the environment in which the body’s experience lives (in terms of genes, this view is correct). However, the chemical modification of genes and histones also leads to the existence of epigenetic phenomena, and the heritable expression of genetic information will be affected by environmental factors. Epigenetics is the most advanced genetic research in the past 20 years, so it is impossible for the author to understand it when writing this book). The genes in my body come from my four grandparents. These genes flow directly from them through my parents and reach me. What my parents have acquired, acquired, learned or experienced will not have any impact on the gene itself when these genes flow through them. On this point, there may be some evil. However, no matter how unchangeable and unshakable these genes are from generation to generation, the shape of the phenotype shown in the body they flow through is not unchangeable and unshakable at all. If I am a homozygous of gene G, nothing can stop me from passing gene G to all my children except mutation. But there are so many things that cannot be changed. As for whether I, or my children, can show the phenotypic characteristics generally associated with having gene G, it often depends more on how we were raised, what kind of food we ate, what kind of education we experienced, and what other genes we happen to have. Therefore, among the two major effects of genes - making more copies of themselves and affecting phenotypes - the first effect is indeed not easy to change (if rare mutations are aside), while the other effect is highly variable. I think the confusion between evolution and development is also partly responsible for the myth of genetic determinism.
Most of this chapter is based on the assumption that a biologist may want to infer the “function” of a behavior pattern within the framework of Darwinism. This does not mean that all behavior patterns must have a Darwinian function. There may be a large class of behavior patterns that are neutral or even harmful to individual organisms in selection, and can not be regarded as the product of natural selection. If this is the case, the discussion in this chapter does not apply to them. However, one expression is very reasonable: “I am very interested in adaptability. I don’t have to think of all behavior patterns as adaptability, but I want to study those behavior patterns that are adaptive. “ Similarly, the expression prefers to study vertebrates rather than invertebrates does not mean that we believe that all animals are vertebrates. Considering that the field of interest is adaptive behavior, when we talk about the Darwinian evolution of the object of interest, it is impossible not to assume a genetic basis in advance. Using “gene for X” as a concise way to talk about “gene basis needed to realize X” is a standard operation of population genetics for more than half a century.

Chapter 11 finding mother: new replication factors
But do we have to go to the distant universe to find other kinds of replication factors and other kinds of subsequent evolutionary phenomena? I think a new type of replication factor has recently appeared on our planet. It was right in front of us, but it was still in its infancy and awkwardly drifting in its original soup. But it is driving the process of evolution, and the speed is beyond the reach of the original factors.
This new soup is the soup of human culture. We need a name for this new replication factor. Let’s study the concept of “God”. We don’t know how it was first produced in the mother seeking library. It probably appeared after many independent “mutation” processes. Anyway, the concept of “God” is really very old. How does it replicate itself? It is reproduced and spread through oral words and written words with the help of great music and great art. Why does it have such high survival value? You should remember that the “survival value” here does not mean the value of genes in the gene pool, but the value of looking for mothers in the mother pool. The real meaning of this question is what gives the stability and permeability of the concept of “God” in the cultural environment. The survival value of God’s mother seeking library comes from its strong psychological appeal“ The concept of “God” provides a seemingly plausible answer to some profound and distressing questions about survival. It implies that all kinds of unfairness in this world can be corrected in the afterlife. God stretched out his “eternal arms” to bear our human defects, just like a placebo prescribed by doctors for patients, which will also produce certain effects due to spiritual effects. This is part of the reason why God is accepted by people and copied and spread in people’s minds from generation to generation. We can say that in the environment provided by human culture, the image of God exists in the form of mother seeking with high survival value or appeal.
Broadly speaking, mother seeking replicates herself by imitation. But just as not all genes that can replicate themselves are good at self replication, some mothers in the mother searching library can achieve greater success than others. This process is similar to natural selection. I have listed some characteristics that help to improve the survival value of mother seeking. But generally speaking, these characteristics must be the same as those of replication factors mentioned in Chapter 2: longevity, fecundity and precise replication ability. The essential condition is that the brain should be able to imitate: then there will be a mother seeking to make full use of this ability.
Human beings may also have an extraordinary feature - the ability to show sincere and selfless altruistic behavior. We have at least the spiritual strength to take care of our long-term selfish interests, not just short-term selfish interests. We are built as genetic machines and trained as mother seeking machines, but we have enough strength to oppose our founders. Chapter 12 a good man is rewarded
No one would think of bacteria as a conscious strategist, but parasitic bacteria seamlessly interact with their hosts in a prisoner’s dilemma.
